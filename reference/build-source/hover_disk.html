The Hover Disk

Materials for a high-χν lift body — choosing the disk substrate that turns vertex-sharing coupling into usable force.
Osika & collaborators — draft 0.1, 2026-06-14.

A hover disk lifts by the χν (vertex-sharing) channel, not by pushing air — so its lift is a materials problem. The χν force factors into a known hardware lever times two material-set numbers: the coupling efficiency η and the body's stored-energy ceiling. A first-pass proxy (an estimate, not a derived law — §4) puts (i) η ≈ vphase/c — the coupling scales as the substrate's coherent-mode phase velocity over c, so acoustic bodies are capped near η ≲ 10−5 while electromagnetic (THz / optical / plasmonic) modes reach η ~ 0.1–0.5; and (ii) the energy ceiling at the material's specific resilience σ²/(Eρ), a size-independent self-lift limit. On those levers bronze loses on both and single-crystal diamond is the strongest single first core — structural champion and high-index optical substrate. But no single material carries all four UFO §8 predicates, so the actual convergent body is a layered stack (§8): a geometry-match register, a coupling/nonlinearity film, a high-η aperture skin, and a phase-coherent boundary. Honest status: the force law is derived in form; its magnitude — Csub·|χν|² — is open, unmeasured, and every χν measurement in the programme to date is null. This paper ranks the substrates that would maximise the three force terms; it does not claim lift — it names what to build and measure. Reproducible numbers: hover/disk_material_ladder.py.

§1What this paper is

How to Build a UFO establishes the architecture: a disk-shaped coherent body that biases how the spacetime lattice settles in its neighbourhood, producing a net force its own mass-energy bookkeeping does not predict (the χν / vertex-sharing channel). The DIY Hoverbike is the ground-effect prototype. This paper takes one question from §8 of the UFO paper and answers it in depth:

Of the materials we can actually obtain, which build a disk that reaches high χν force — and why?

This is an engineering/materials paper. It does not claim a force; it states the force law, isolates the two numbers the material sets, and ranks the candidate substrates against them. Every quantity is tagged derived, estimate, or open (bench-measured). The numbers are reproducible from hover/disk_material_ladder.py.

How to read the tags (and where the honesty lives). derived — follows from the force law or the geometry (the form F = −∇Eint, the bound g ≤ 1/√3). estimate — a heuristic proxy, a prior not a wall (notably η ≈ vphase/c, §4). open — a number only the bench fixes (notably the magnitude Csub·|χν|², currently null on every substrate measured). bench — a build-and-measure step. No quantity in this paper is a demonstrated force; the paper ranks candidates to test, and the directional derivative ∂|χν|²/∂X (not η alone) is what a symmetric high-η disk would still need to be non-zero to lift at all (§3, §14.4).

§2Why the lift is a materials problem, not an acoustics problem

A natural first instinct is to lift the disk acoustically — hold a trapped high-pressure region beneath it. That works in ground effect and only there. Steady hover requires a steady downward flux of momentum through any surface around the craft (Newton's third law). A pressure cushion routes that momentum into the ground, which is why it is cheap — but remove the ground and a genuinely-trapped, static overpressure exerts zero net force (internal forces cancel; you cannot lift yourself by pressurising a tank you carry), while a leaky one is just "throw air down" in disguise, with a power floor of tens of kW (a coherent jet) to ~500 kW (radiated sound) for a person-scale craft.

Free-altitude hover needs a non-ground momentum sink. A pressure cushion's reaction momentum terminates on the Earth, so cushion lift is intrinsically a ground-effect regime. The only altitude-independent momentum sink in the framework is the lattice background itself — momentum "redistributed into and out of the lattice" (UFO §4). That sink is the χν channel. Hence a free-flying disk lifts by χν, and how much force it makes is set by how well its material couples to the lattice.

So the disk's lift is a property of its substrate, not its aerodynamics. The rest of this paper is about choosing that substrate.

§3The force law and the three things a material sets

§4Lever 1 (dominant) — coupling: the η ≈ vphase/c proxy

The substrate aggregate (CHI_NU_AS_VERTEX_SHARING_STRENGTH.md) is the mode-overlap of the substrate's coherent mode with the lattice mode at the drive frequency, η = λsub/λlat. Since λlat = c/f and λsub·f = vphase (the substrate mode's phase velocity), a first-pass overlap estimate collapses to a single ratio — a heuristic proxy, not a derived law:

\( \boxed{\;\eta \;\approx\; \dfrac{\lambda_{\rm sub}}{\lambda_{\rm lat}} \;=\; \dfrac{v_{\rm phase}}{c}\;} \)
Heuristic (a prior, not a wall). To first order η tracks the substrate's coherent-mode phase velocity over c: a vibration whose phase fronts move near light-speed overlaps the lattice mode coherently across the body; a slow vibration oscillates many times under one lattice wavefront and the overlap cancels. The whole "wavelength-mismatch suppression" the bench papers warn about is this ratio. But the derived object is the χν overlap integral (CHI_NU_AS_VERTEX_SHARING_STRENGTH.md); this vphase/c form is only its leading-order proxy, and the true η is open (bench-measured).

4.1 The four open multipliers (UFO §8 predicates)

On top of the naive vphase/c value sit multipliers that the framework predicts but only the bench can size. Each is a way to make the overlap add constructively beyond the bare velocity ratio:

  • P1 — icosahedral symmetry match. A substrate whose own structural symmetry is the lattice's primitive (icosahedral) cell has an overlap integral that accumulates rather than cancels across the body.
  • P2 — a coupling channel that ties spin to the lattice. Multiferroic order gives two channels in concert — but its rigorous form is spin–orbit coupling (the operator λ L·S), the only interaction that mechanically fuses spin to real-space orbital/lattice motion. SOC strength λ is the natural figure of merit; the 5d iridate Jeff=1/2 state (λ ≈ 0.5 eV) maxes it. See §12.
  • P3 — phase-coherent condensate at the boundary. A Cooper-pair / Bose / optical condensate preserves coupling phase across the body; a thermal boundary randomises it. Its one room-temperature realisation is the magnon (spin-wave) Bose-Einstein condensate — a body-spanning, single-phase spin condensate (§12).
  • P4 — drive frequency near the lattice mode. This is the vphase/c term itself — the reason THz-to-optical drive is the framework's prescription.
The sizes of P1–P3 are the open numbers. The naive η = vphase/c is a lower-bound-flavoured prior that assumes none of them. A lattice-matched, multiferroic, condensate-boundary body could have η well above its bare velocity ratio — which is exactly why the substrate ladder is an experimental program and not a closed calculation. (Anti-pessimism guardrail: a low naive η is a prior, not a wall.)

4.2 The light-consuming corollary — a cheap first-pass filter

If coupling to the lattice goes as the substrate's electromagnetic mode-overlap, then a material that couples optical-frequency EM into a coherent mode is, in the visible band, dark — the same overlap that biases the lattice also absorbs light. Absorptance should track |χν(ω)|² in the coupling band. (This is the optical face of the coupling developed in how_to_build_a_ufo.html §13.11, and reconciles Lazar's paired report of a craft that "consumes light and bends gravity" — two frequencies of one high-frequency mode-overlap, not two marvels.)

"Is it anomalously, broadbandly black at its coupling band?" becomes a cheap screen. A good χν optical coupler must be unusually light-consuming; if a candidate is optically ordinary, the high-η reading is falsified. Strikingly, this filter points straight at the §7 top picks — graphene's opacity is literally the fine-structure constant (πα = 2.3% per layer, broadband, gate-tunable; Nair 2008), CNT forests are the blackest known material (>99.99%), and the Pt-group "blacks" (including iridium) sit just behind. The honest null to keep in mind: a passive super-absorber (a Vantablack-class coating) is black without any χν coupling, so blackness is a necessary screen, not a sufficient proof — it filters candidates in, it does not confirm them.

§5Lever 2 — energy ceiling: specific resilience σ²/(Eρ)

Udrive is stored coherent energy, not dissipated power — so cavity Q raises efficiency (you reach a given energy at lower power) but not the ceiling. For a body that stores the energy mechanically, the ceiling is set by the stress at which it fractures or fatigues. Integrating the elastic energy density σ²/(2E) over the volume that participates near peak stress (a flexural mode reaches the working stress only at the surface antinodes) gives

\( U_{\rm cap} \;=\; \dfrac{\sigma_{\rm work}^2\,V}{12\,E} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \dfrac{U_{\rm cap}}{\text{mass}} \;=\; \dfrac{\sigma_{\rm work}^2}{12\,E\rho} \)

So the relevant material figure of merit is the specific resilience R = σwork²/(Eρ) — the elastic energy a material stores per unit weight. It sets a clean, size-independent ceiling on χν self-lift at perfect coupling:

\( \left(\dfrac{F}{W}\right)_{\rm perfect} \;=\; \dfrac{\sigma_{\rm work}^2\,\kappa\,g_{\max}^2}{12\,E\rho\,g\,L_{\rm grad}} \;\propto\; R \)
The body's mass cancels. A bigger disk is heavier in exact proportion, leaving only a weak 1/Lgrad (∝ 1/radius) residual. So whether a material can self-lift via χν — even at perfect coupling — is a property of the material, not the size.

Material and drive-mode choice alone close ~13–14 orders of magnitude of the χν lift gap. The disk does not need a coupling miracle; it needs the right body driven in the right mode, and then the bench decides whether the open factors land it above or below unity. (The optical figure conservatively uses the mechanical energy ceiling; wide-bandgap diamond's optical-damage ceiling is plausibly higher.)

§7The best options, in detail

Each class below is rated on the two levers — coupling η and energy ceiling R — plus which open predicates it can carry. No single material maxes everything; §8 assembles them.

7.1 Single-crystal diamond — the convergent single body

Diamond is the one material that wins both levers outright. It is the structural champion (highest sound speed of any material, 17 km/s; fracture strength of CVD diamond ~2 GPa; specific resilience 1084 J/kg, self-lift 3.07), and simultaneously a high-index optical substrate (n = 2.42 → η ≈ 0.41 in its optical mode). It has the highest known thermal conductivity (it sheds drive heat), a wide bandgap (high optical-damage threshold, so the optical energy ceiling is comfortable), and lab-grown CVD plates are now manufacturable at centimetre scale and growing. A diamond disk reaches the electromagnetic-η regime without a separate active layer and holds the energy in the same body. If you build one disk from one material, build it from diamond.

7.2 Silicon carbide — the scalable second

SiC (4H polytype) is diamond's cheaper, larger-wafer cousin: high sound speed (11.8 km/s), optical substrate (n = 2.6 → η ≈ 0.38), mature wafer and CVD manufacturing at 150–200 mm. Its specific resilience is lower (173 J/kg, self-lift 0.49 at 1 m), so a SiC disk is a strong η body that may need a high-R backing layer to clear self-lift. The natural prototype substrate before diamond is affordable at disk scale.

7.3 Titanium, maraging steel, CFRP — the structural cores

These win Lever 2 but only reach acoustic η on their own (~10⁻⁵). Their role is the structural core that holds the coherent energy beneath an active η-skin. Ti-6Al-4V (R 495, self-lift 1.40) is the aerospace default: weldable, available, fatigue-characterised. Maraging steel (R 947, self-lift 2.68) trades density for a higher ceiling. CFRP has the highest specific resilience of all (1667 J/kg, self-lift 4.72) — but it is anisotropic and a fibre/matrix composite, so its coherent-mode Q and phase coherence are poor; treat it as a mass-efficient backing, not a coherent substrate.

7.4 Icosahedral quasicrystals — the symmetry layer (P1)

Al-Pd-Mn and Al-Cu-Fe icosahedral quasicrystals (Shechtman 1984; Tsai 1987) are the only substrate class whose own diffraction symmetry is the lattice's icosahedral primitive cell. That is predicate P1 in material form: a potentially large, open multiplier on η from symmetry-matched overlap. They are brittle (low R), so they serve as a thin symmetry layer on a structural core, not as the load body. This is the substrate rung that tests whether the symmetry-match multiplier is real and large.

7.5 Multiferroics (BiFeO₃) — the coupling film (P2 + P4)

Bismuth ferrite carries simultaneous ferroelectric and antiferromagnetic order (two coupling channels, P2) and supports THz electromagnon modes (P4, η into the ~0.1 regime). Cycloid-suppressed epitaxial thin films reach the strongest room-temperature magnetoelectric coupling known (~3 V/cm·Oe; Catalan & Scott 2009). Grown as a thin film on the structural/symmetry core, BiFeO₃ is the active coupling layer that drives the body into the electromagnetic-η regime through a broken-symmetry order parameter rather than through bulk motion.

7.6 Superconducting condensates — the coherent boundary (P3)

A phase-coherent condensate at the boundary preserves coupling phase across the whole body (P3) — the lineage of the Ning-Li and Tajmar rotating-superconductor experiments (UFO §9). Cuprate films give P3 at 77 K; MgB₂ at ~39 K. Bismuth is the standout: its 0.53 mK superconductivity is six orders below any BCS prediction and has no accepted mechanism (Prakash et al. 2017), so it may escape the s-wave Canterbury-2008 bound that constrains conventional superconductors. The condensate is a surface boundary layer, not the structural body.

7.7 Photonic and plasmonic skins — the η extreme

The highest η of any buildable substrate. Silicon photonic (n ≈ 3.48, η ≈ 0.29) is the optical-chamber substrate class; a metallic plasmonic skin reaches vphase ~ 0.5c (η ≈ 0.5). Energy is stored in the optical/plasmonic field (high damage ceiling), not as mechanical strain, so Lever 2 is comfortable. These are not structural bodies — they are the η reference point and the natural "active surface" of a diamond/SiC disk.

7.8 Wildcard — ORMUS / monatomic Pt-group

If the claimed high-spin-nucleus monatomic forms of Au/Ir/Rh are real (UFO §11.9), they would supply predicate P3 (a phase-coherent condensate) at room temperature, removing the cryogenic constraint. Independent replication of the chemistry is the missing receipt; listed for completeness, not relied upon.

7.10 Graphene — the resilience champion (Lever 2 by ~7000×)

Graphene is the highest-specific-resilience material known: σ ≈ 130 GPa, E ≈ 1 TPa, ρ ≈ 2267 kg/m³ give R ≈ 7.5×10⁶ J/kg — about 7000× diamond (single-wall CNTs match it at the theoretical limit; real CNT fibre ~4× lower). Its energy ceiling is so high that the self-lift question inverts: at perfect coupling it would lift ~7000× its weight, so the only thing that matters is how much η it can reach. The honest range (self-lift = (F/W)perfect × η²):

Graphene's η is the open, contested number. The vF = 0.003c Dirac velocity is a quasiparticle transport speed, not a coherent mode's phase velocity — do not credit graphene with EM-band η on its strength. And the extreme plasmon confinement that makes graphene exciting also makes those plasmons slow (confinement and high η pull opposite ways). The best honest η is the graphene–hBN hybrid plasmon-phonon polariton (~0.1–0.3, and that is a group velocity). But because R is ~7000× diamond, even the pessimistic slow-plasmon η self-lifts ~0.2× weight on the naive estimate — graphene is robust to η uncertainty in a way diamond is not.

Three further framework hits make carbon hard to ignore: (i) 30°-twisted bilayer graphene is a genuine dodecagonal (12-fold) quasicrystal — predicate P1, and a direct match to the project's 12-port / icosahedral geometry; (ii) magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene superconducts (Tc ≈ 1.7 K, geometry-set) — predicate P3, a condensate switched by a twist angle, conceptually resonant with vertex-sharing; (iii) graphene's opacity is the fine-structure constant (§4.2). Caveats: graphene has essentially no native P2 (weak spin–orbit, non-magnetic — needs proximity engineering), MATBG's Tc is cryogenic and fabrication-fragile, and graphene aerogel/foam is a trap — going ultralight collapses σ faster than ρ, dropping R ~100× below monolayer. Rank by σ²/(Eρ), never by density.

7.10b Carbon nanotubes — the light-consuming aperture and the spin handle

CNTs share graphene's single-tube resilience (~7×10⁶ J/kg) but lose it in any buildable form (fibre/yarn/buckypaper fall to 0.01–1.2×10⁶ J/kg as tubes slip on their van-der-Waals contacts) — so CNTs are not the Lever-2 win. They earn their place on two CNT-specific axes graphene cannot match:

  • The light-consuming aperture (decisive). A vertically-aligned CNT forest is the blackest known material — Vantablack ~99.965%, the MIT 2019 record ≥99.995%, broadband UV→THz and angle-independent — because it multiplies intrinsic 1D broadband absorption by geometric light-trapping. That product is the physical realisation of the §4.2 filter: the forest forces every entering mode to sample an enormous number of surface vertices before it can leave — structurally the same "coherent aggregation over many boundary vertices" as the χν overlap sum. A flat graphene sheet has the intrinsic coupling but none of the geometric aggregation (2.3% vs 99.995%). So if a good optical coupler must be black, the CNT forest is the substrate that operationalises the coupling aperture, and graphene is not.
  • Curvature spin–orbit coupling (P2). Wrapping graphene into a tube breaks its mirror symmetry and turns on first-order SOC — measured at 0.37–3.4 meV (a built-in ~29 T axial field), i.e. 10³–10⁴× flat graphene's ~1 µeV, and geometry-tunable by diameter and chirality. CNTs also carry native 1D superconductivity (intrinsic at 0.4 nm, Tc ≈ 15 K; plus rope and proximity SC) — a P3 channel graphene lacks natively — and a quantized Luttinger-liquid plasmon at η ≈ 0.008–0.01 (carrier-density-independent, cleaner than a 2D plasmon). Chirality is also a selectable P1 knob.
Two honest caveats. (1) The blackness→χν inference is the framework's own postulate — a forest's blackness is fully explained by ordinary absorption into heat, with no χν required; it is a necessary screen, not a proof. (2) The morphologies fight: the 99.995%-black forest is a fragile low-fill-fraction array, while a load-bearing disc wants dense fibre — you cannot get both the black aperture and the structural body from one piece. On the "Sport-Model black panels" lore: it is not documented — Lazar's craft was described as bare metallic, and the famous stealth blacks are radar (magnetic-loss) absorbers, a different mechanism from optical ultra-black. The CNT-aperture case rests on the laboratory blackest-material record alone, which is enough.

7.11 Iridates — the coupling oxides (it's the compound, not the metal)

Iridium-the-metal is a poor candidate: dense and brittle (intrinsic Frank-loop cleavage), specific resilience ≤ steel (R ≈ 336 J/kg at cold-worked strength), acoustic-only coupling, and superconducting only at 0.11 K. The framework-relevant physics lives entirely in the iridates (Ir-oxides), one of the richest playgrounds for exotic coherent modes:

  • Sr₂IrO₄ — a spin–orbit-coupled Jeff=1/2 Mott insulator (a near-perfect cuprate analogue) with a giant magnetoelectric effect (predicate P2, two coupling channels via an unconventional SOC mechanism).
  • THz collective modes — dispersing magnons up to ~50 THz plus a spin–orbit exciton, intrinsically in the high-frequency band (P4), and strongly strain-tunable — i.e. the coupling mode is itself a handle that ties Lever 1 to Lever 2.
  • A nuclear↔electronic channel — ¹⁹¹Ir was the original Mössbauer nuclide; ¹⁹³Ir nuclear-resonant scattering reads the magnetic state, and a giant magnetic isotope effect appears in an Ir quantum-spin-liquid. A genuine second coupling channel few elements offer.
  • Honeycomb/pyrochlore iridates host Kitaev-spin-liquid and Weyl-semimetal states (P1-flavoured frustrated geometry), though all real iridates order magnetically rather than realising the ideal spin liquid.

Caveat: predicate P3 is unfilled for iridates (no confirmed iridate superconductor yet), and iridium is among the rarest, most expensive elements — a practical ceiling on any build. Use the oxide as a thin coupling layer, pair it with a separate condensate boundary for P3, and treat the ORMUS / "monatomic-iridium" claims as a testable lead rather than established physics — see §7.12.

7.12 ORMUS / monatomic iridium — extracting the testable claim

Breakthrough materials have a real history of being dismissed before they were understood, so the right posture toward a fringe lead is not "ignore it" but "extract the falsifiable physical claim and run the cheapest decisive test." Doing that for ORMUS iridium separates a discardable myth from a genuinely untested sliver.

What does not survive. The headline claims — bulk room-temperature superconductivity with Meissner levitation, ~4/9 weight loss / "weighs less than nothing," arc-made permanent high-spin nuclei — are contradicted: a room-temperature superconductor is trivially SQUID-detectable and none has been shown; the weight anomaly is the signature of a thermogravimetric buoyancy artifact; and superdeformed/high-spin nuclei are picosecond–nanosecond accelerator states, not a storable powder. Commercial "ORMUS" assays as mostly magnesium hydroxide with no significant precious metal. That part is noise.

What is real, and untested for this material. Hudson's core intuition — a noble-metal atom stripped of bulk metallic bonding occupies an anomalous d-hole electronic state with new magnetism — was vindicated in spirit by mainstream nanoscience he never saw: thiol-capped gold nanoparticles show room-temperature ferromagnetic hysteresis attributed to 5d localized holes (Crespo, PRL 2004) — bulk-diamagnetic gold becoming magnetic when its surface bonding is disrupted. Single-atom Ir catalysts and Au "superatom" clusters are real and electronically unlike the bulk. None of this is superconductivity or antigravity — but it is a legitimate P2 (spin / d-hole) channel, and crucially no one has put a genuine Hudson-route Ir/Rh preparation through a SQUID + EPR + microwave-surface-impedance battery and published it. For that narrow claim, "the evidence isn't in yet" is literally true.
The cheapest decisive experiment is one afternoon on a shared SQUID/VSM (5–400 K, field sweep): it falsifies the superconductivity claim outright if null, and detects any real room-temperature moment (Crespo-scale) down to ~10⁻⁷ emu — pinning whether the prep carries an anomalous P2 spin channel. Pair with EPR (high-spin signature) and a buoyancy-blank thermogravimetry run (kills the weight claim). And if the actual goal is a room-temperature condensate (P3), the honest place to look is not the powder but driven organic exciton-polariton condensates, which already deliver coherent matter at 300 K in peer-reviewed work and fit the high-frequency-drive predicate P4. Investigate the fringe by measuring it.

§11Diamagnetic levitation — the conventional benchmark (and the Meissner→P3 bridge)

One conventional, room-temperature, fully-demonstrated mechanism does lift ordinary matter — including living tissue — and it is the honest yardstick every χν claim should be measured against: diamagnetic levitation (Geim's levitating frog; Berry & Geim 1997). Two features make this the right benchmark — and a partial bridge into this paper's framework. The cost scales with volume: small objects levitate over ordinary NdFeB if they are strong diamagnets — pyrolytic graphite, the best room-temperature diamagnet, levitates over permanent magnets with no power at all (the cheap demonstrator) — while a human needs ≈40 T over ~1 GW of coil power (or a superconducting persistent magnet, no steady power).

13.1 The exact lattice is forbidden in flat space — by your own paper, and by theorem

The universe lattice has four defining features: equal edges, degree-3 vertices, pentagon smallest loops, and dodecahedral cells with icosahedral symmetry (φ-forced). No ordinary crystal can carry all of them, for two independent reasons:

  • The crystallographic restriction theorem. Periodic crystals admit only 2-, 3-, 4-, 6-fold rotational symmetry — 5-fold and icosahedral are forbidden. (This is precisely why quasicrystals were revolutionary, and why no periodic element can carry the lattice's symmetry.)
No element's crystal reproduces the exact infinite lattice in flat space.   • Degree-3 is carbon's signature. sp² carbon makes exactly three σ-bonds — it is the three-way-vertex element. Graphene is a degree-3 net.
  • Dodecahedrane (C₂₀H₂₀) is the literal primitive cell. Twenty carbons at the twenty degree-3 corners of a regular dodecahedron, each bonded to three carbons — exactly shape §5's "twenty corners; at every corner three pentagons meet." 14.1 Is there an insulating quasicrystal? (Mostly no — use a photonic one)

The realizable route to "insulating + 12-fold" is therefore a photonic quasicrystal: pattern a genuine dielectric (diamond, SiN, Si) in a 12-fold tiling, so the quasiperiodicity is geometric while the material stays a true insulator (complete photonic gaps are demonstrated even at low index). 14.4 Propellantless, not reactionless — the line this whole programme walks

This deserves to be stated exactly, because it is the line between the legitimate target and the crackpot trap, and they are easy to confuse. The disk's goal is a propellantless drive — no ejected reaction mass, no air thrown down, no propellant tank ("massless propulsion"). That is allowed. What is forbidden is a reactionless drive — a force with no reaction at all — because momentum conservation (Noether's theorem, translational symmetry) is not negotiable.

Propellantless ≠ reactionless. A propellantless drive still has a reaction; it just doesn't carry its own reaction mass. The momentum goes into a field or medium that carries it away — photons for a solar sail, the rails for a maglev, and, for this programme, the spacetime lattice. That is the entire content of the χν proposal (UFO §4: "momentum redistributed into and out of the lattice background, which carries it away"). So "charge up the substrate's coherent energy until a force appears" is exactly the force law F = −∇[Csub|χν|²Udrive] — propellantless, and momentum-conserving because the lattice is the sink.

This is why the Buhler "thrust persists after the voltage is removed" claim is a red flag and the χν disk is not: the red flag is specifically a claim of force with no identifiable sink (force from a static stored charge, going nowhere) — that would violate conservation. The χν programme avoids the flag by naming the sink (the lattice) up front. The honest test that separates the two is always the same single question: where does the reaction momentum go? A legitimate propellantless drive can answer it; a reactionless claim cannot. For this disk the answer is "the lattice" — and whether that coupling is real, and carries momentum, is precisely the open bench question the whole substrate hunt (§§4–13) is in service of. The programme is on the right side of the line if the χν coupling exists; that "if" is the experiment, not a claim.

15.1 Buhler's vacuum / "persists-unplugged" claim — the discriminating test

Separately from the patent, Buhler claims in interviews (2026) ~2,000 vacuum test-articles showing 5–10 mN of thrust that persists after the power is disconnected — "if the field persists, the force persists" — and states he cannot explain it and that the capacitor "does not drain as a simple energy calculation would predict." This is unreviewed and unreplicated, and it conflicts directly with the independent, peer-reviewed vacuum nulls (Talley, Campbell, Tajmar). It is worth taking seriously precisely because it is double-edged:

  • It is what our force law predicts. F = (Csub|χν|²)·Udrive·κ/Lgrad scales with stored coherent energy, not input power — so a force that persists as long as the charged/coherent state persists (unplugged) is the expected signature of a propellantless, stored-energy drive, not an anomaly.
  • It is also the signature of the single hardest artifact. A charged body in a chamber electrostatically couples to the (conductive) walls, and that coupling — plus thermal/outgassing drift from a warm charged device — also produces a force that persists with the trapped charge, unplugged. Buhler's described controls (ion wind, Faraday cage) do not exclude wall-coupling, thermal, or outgassing forces. "Persists unplugged" does not, by itself, distinguish a lattice coupling from trapped-charge-to-chamber coupling.
The clean discriminator (and it's the control Buhler is missing): a χν force couples to the lattice, which is everywhere — so it must be independent of the chamber. A trapped-charge artifact couples to the chamber walls — so it must change when you change the chamber. The decisive test is therefore: hold the device and its charge state fixed, and vary the chamber (wall distance, geometry, grounded vs floating). If the force is unchanged → it is not wall-coupling (the χν/lattice reading survives). If it tracks the chamber → it is the artifact. Add thermal/outgassing nulls (dummy heated mass) and the energy-balance audit (does the stored field energy actually drain as work is done?). This is exactly the experiment §15's bound says is missing — and it is the one that would either promote Buhler's claim or explain it away.

15.2 What the Buhler program contributes — and the lever it leaves on the table

Read constructively (from his 2026 long-form interview), the program converges with this paper in three useful ways, and reveals one lever it has not touched — which is exactly where this framework adds value.

  • His mechanism is conservation-respecting real physics, not "force from nothing." Buhler frames it as "the conversion from field momentum to mechanical momentum… you can convert momentum stored in the field into real momentum. They've done that in the '70s with the angular momentum, but the linear analog was hindered by… hidden momentum, so I tried to find systems with no hidden momentum." That is the Feynman-disk / Shockley–James hidden-momentum lineage — the EM field genuinely carries momentum, and the claim is a geometry that converts it without the cancellation that normally forbids the linear case. This is the same propellantless-not-reactionless logic as §14.4, anchored in conventional field-momentum physics: the field is the reservoir (and in this paper's frame, the lattice is the deeper reservoir).
  • His material wishlist is our §14 FOM — and he independently reaches for bismuth. He wants "new materials with high dielectric breakdown strength" plus high permittivity (the high-K dielectric was "a really important factor for determining the thrust") — i.e. the energy-density FOM u = ½εrε0Ebd². He has tried barium titanate ("good high-permittivity powder," unremarkable) and is keen to test bismuth — converging on the same element our §7.6 / §9 threads flag.
  • His controls are real: a lifter sealed in a box on a scale flatlines (ion wind cancels, conservation intact) while ~200 µN persists — he uses the ion-wind null itself as the discriminator, plus Faraday shielding, thruster-off-the-scale spacing, charge neutralisation between runs, and sign-flip tests.
The unexplored lever is frequency — and it is the one this framework says matters most. Buhler is DC-only: "we're still exploring the DC versions… we haven't explored all the different ways you can apply different voltages and currents… a lot of room in the future." His interviewer notes DC is "a really long wavelength excitation" (ω → 0). But §4's coupling lever is η ≈ vphase/c, which rises with drive frequency — DC sits at the bottom of it. So this framework makes a concrete, additive prediction Buhler has not tested: driving the same asymmetric high-K dielectric structure at RF → THz (rather than DC) should raise the coupling by orders of magnitude. Frequency is the knob his program has left on the table and the one our η-lever points straight at.
